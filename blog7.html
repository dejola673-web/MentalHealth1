Understanding and treating different types of eating disorders..

January 2024 10 min read Dr. John Stewart
Eating disorders are serious mental health conditions that affect millions of people worldwide. They involve persistent disturbances in eating behaviors and distressing thoughts and emotions about food, weight, and body image. Understanding the different types of eating disorders and available treatments is crucial for recovery. With proper help and support, full recovery is possible.

Need Immediate Support?

If you're in crisis, call the National Eating Disorders Association (NEDA) Helpline at 1-800-931-2237 or text "NEDA" to 741741. For life-threatening emergencies, call 911 immediately.

What Are Eating Disorders?

Eating disorders are complex mental health conditions that can affect anyone, regardless of age, gender, race, or body size. They're characterized by severe disturbances in eating behaviors and related thoughts and emotions. These aren't simply about food—they often serve as ways to cope with emotional pain, stress, or feelings of lack of control.

Eating disorders have the highest mortality rate of any mental illness, making early intervention critical. They affect approximately 9% of the population worldwide and can lead to serious physical complications affecting the heart, bones, digestive system, and more. However, with proper treatment and support, recovery is absolutely possible.

  • Preoccupation with food, weight, and body shape
  • Restrictive eating or overeating patterns
  • Distorted body image perceptions
  • Secretive eating behaviors
  • Physical symptoms like fatigue and dizziness
  • Social withdrawal and isolation

Major Types of Eating Disorders

Anorexia Nervosa

Characterized by extreme food restriction, intense fear of weight gain, and distorted body image. Includes restricting and binge-eating/purging subtypes.

Bulimia Nervosa

Involves cycles of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors like vomiting, excessive exercise, or laxative use to prevent weight gain.

Binge Eating Disorder

Recurrent episodes of eating large quantities of food with a sense of loss of control, but without regular compensatory behaviors.

ARFID

Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder involves limited food intake due to sensory sensitivities, lack of interest in eating, or fear of consequences of eating.

Rumination Disorder

Repeated regurgitation of food that may be re-chewed, re-swallowed, or spit out, occurring for at least one month and not due to a medical condition.

Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder (OSFED)

Includes atypical presentations that cause significant distress but don't meet full criteria for other eating disorders. Still serious and require treatment.

Recognize the Warning Signs Early

Early detection significantly improves recovery outcomes. Watch for warning signs including dramatic weight loss or fluctuation, preoccupation with food and calories, ritualistic eating behaviors, excessive exercise, frequent bathroom trips after meals, wearing baggy clothes to hide body shape, and social withdrawal from activities involving food. Physical signs may include dizziness, fainting, feeling cold, stomach problems, sleep difficulties, concentration problems, and irregular or absent menstrual periods. If you notice these signs in yourself or a loved one, reach out for professional help immediately.

Understanding the Root Causes

Eating disorders don't have a single cause—they develop from a complex interaction of genetic, biological, psychological, and sociocultural factors. Understanding these contributing factors can help reduce stigma and guide treatment approaches.

  • Genetic predisposition and family history
  • Brain chemistry and neurobiology differences
  • Psychological factors like perfectionism and anxiety
  • History of trauma or abuse
  • Societal pressure and media influence
  • Major life transitions or stressful events

Seek Comprehensive Medical Assessment

Eating disorders can cause serious medical complications that require monitoring and treatment. Schedule a complete medical evaluation that includes assessment of vital signs, heart function, bone density, nutritional status, and any other affected systems. Medical stabilization is often the first step in treatment, as physical health complications can be life-threatening. Your treatment team should include medical doctors, psychiatrists, therapists, and nutritionists who work together to address all aspects of the disorder. Don't let shame or fear prevent you from getting the medical care you need—healthcare providers are there to help, not judge.

Evidence-Based Treatment Approaches

Family-Based Treatment (FBT)

Also called the Maudsley Approach, FBT is highly effective for adolescents. Parents take an active role in helping restore their child's weight and eating patterns before gradually returning control to the teen.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

CBT for eating disorders (CBT-E) helps identify and change negative thought patterns about food, weight, and body image, while developing healthier coping strategies.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)

Particularly helpful for binge eating disorder and bulimia, DBT teaches skills for emotional regulation, distress tolerance, and mindfulness to manage urges and emotions.

Nutritional Counseling

Working with a registered dietitian specialized in eating disorders helps normalize eating patterns, challenge food rules, and develop a healthy relationship with food and nutrition.

Choose the Right Level of Care

Treatment for eating disorders exists on a spectrum from outpatient to inpatient care. Outpatient therapy involves regular sessions while living at home—ideal for those medically stable. Intensive Outpatient Programs (IOP) provide several hours of treatment multiple days per week. Partial Hospitalization Programs (PHP) offer full-day treatment with return home at night. Residential treatment provides 24/7 care in a home-like setting for those needing intensive support. Inpatient hospitalization is reserved for medical emergencies and severe cases requiring constant monitoring. Your treatment team will help determine the most appropriate level of care based on medical stability, psychological symptoms, and social support.

Supporting Your Recovery Journey

Challenge Negative Thoughts

Learn to identify and question the eating disorder voice. Keep a thought journal to track distorted beliefs about food, weight, and body image, then work with your therapist to develop more balanced perspectives.

Build a Support Network

Recovery is difficult to do alone. Connect with support groups, trusted friends and family who understand eating disorders, and online communities. Having people who can support you during difficult moments is crucial.

Limit Triggering Media

Unfollow social media accounts that promote diet culture, compare bodies, or trigger negative feelings. Curate your feed with body-positive, recovery-focused content that supports your healing journey.

Develop Healthy Coping Skills

Replace eating disorder behaviors with healthier ways to manage emotions: deep breathing, journaling, art, music, talking to a friend, or engaging in activities you enjoy. Create a toolbox of alternatives to turn to when urges arise.

Practice Self-Compassion and Patience

Recovery is not linear—there will be setbacks, and that's completely normal. Treat yourself with the same kindness you would offer a friend going through a difficult time. Recognize that healing takes time and that every small step forward matters. Celebrate victories like challenging a fear food, eating with others, or tolerating uncomfortable emotions without using eating disorder behaviors. Be patient with yourself on difficult days and remember that setbacks are opportunities to learn, not signs of failure. Recovery is possible, and you deserve to fully heal and live a life free from the constraints of an eating disorder.

Stages of Recovery

1
Awareness

Recognizing that you have an eating disorder and acknowledging that you need help. This requires courage and honesty.

2
Seeking Help

Reaching out to professionals and building your treatment team. This includes medical doctors, therapists, and dietitians.

3
Medical Stabilization

Addressing immediate physical health concerns and nutritional rehabilitation to restore body to a healthy state.

4
Active Treatment

Engaging in therapy to address underlying psychological issues, develop coping skills, and challenge eating disorder thoughts.

5
Maintenance

Continuing to practice recovery skills, managing triggers, and preventing relapse while rebuilding your life.

6
Thriving

Living a full life where the eating disorder no longer controls your choices, relationships, or sense of self-worth.

Create a Relapse Prevention Plan

As you progress in recovery, work with your treatment team to develop a relapse prevention plan. Identify your personal warning signs that you might be slipping back into eating disorder behaviors. List healthy coping strategies you can use when you notice these signs. Include emergency contacts for when you need immediate support. Establish regular check-ins with your treatment team even when you're doing well. Remember that having a plan doesn't mean you'll relapse—it means you're prepared to catch any slips early and get back on track quickly. Many people in recovery find that having this safety net actually reduces anxiety and helps them feel more confident in their ability to maintain their progress.

Medical Emergencies Requiring Immediate Help

  • Fainting, dizziness, or extreme weakness
  • Irregular heartbeat or chest pain
  • Vomiting blood or severe stomach pain
  • Thoughts of suicide or self-harm
  • Severe dehydration or electrolyte imbalances
  • Rapid, significant weight loss

Common Myths About Eating Disorders

MYTH

"Eating disorders only affect teenage girls and young women."

TRUTH

Eating disorders affect people of all ages, genders, races, and socioeconomic backgrounds. Men, older adults, and people in larger bodies can and do develop eating disorders.

MYTH

"You can tell if someone has an eating disorder by looking at them."

TRUTH

People with eating disorders come in all shapes and sizes. You cannot determine the severity of an eating disorder based on someone's weight or appearance. Many people suffering from serious eating disorders are in "normal" or larger bodies.

MYTH

"Eating disorders are really just about vanity and wanting to look good."

TRUTH

Eating disorders are serious mental illnesses with biological, psychological, and social causes. They're often about control, coping with emotions, trauma, or perfectionism—not simply about appearance.

MYTH

"People with eating disorders just need to eat normally—it's a choice."

TRUTH

Eating disorders involve complex changes in brain function and are not a choice or matter of willpower. Recovery requires professional treatment addressing medical, nutritional, and psychological aspects.

Recovery is Possible—You Deserve to Heal

Living with an eating disorder can feel isolating and hopeless, but recovery is absolutely achievable with the right support and treatment. Thousands of people have recovered from eating disorders and gone on to live fulfilling lives free from the constraints of disordered eating. Recovery takes time, courage, and commitment, but you don't have to do it alone. Whether you're just beginning to acknowledge a problem or you've been struggling for years, it's never too late to seek help. You deserve to have a healthy relationship with food, to feel comfortable in your body, and to live a life defined by your values and passions—not by an eating disorder.
